During the whole course of ages, we find only one woman, and that, Lampido, the Laced¾monian, who was the daughter of a king, the wife of a king, and the mother of a king.[1] Berenice was the only woman who was daughter, sister, and mother of conquerors in the Olympian games,[2] The family of the Curios[3] has been the only one to produce three orators in succession; that of the Fabii alone has given three chiefs of the senate in succession, Fabius Ambustus, his son Fabius Rullianus, and his grandson Quintus Fabius Gurges.[4]

3. He alludes to the three persons, father, son, and grandson, known by the name of C. Scribonius Curio. The first was pr¾tor B.C. 121, one of the most distinguished orators of his time. His son, who acquired some reputation as an orator, was tribune of the people B.C. 90, pr¾tor B.C. 82, and consul in B.C. 76, with Cn. Octavius. He is represented as being possessed of great eloquence, and of extreme purity and brilliancy of diction, but to have had none of the other requisites of an orator. Like his son, he enjoyed the friendship of Cicero. The younger Curio was an orator of great talents, which, from want of industry, he left uncultivated. Cicero endeavoured to direct his talents into a proper channel, but all in vain, and he remained to the end a man of worthless and profligate character. He was married to Fulvia, who afterwards became the wife of Antony.
