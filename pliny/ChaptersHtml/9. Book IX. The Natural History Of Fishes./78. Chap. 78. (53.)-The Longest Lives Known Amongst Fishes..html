CHAP. 78. (53.)ÑTHE LONGEST LIVES KNOWN AMONGST FISHES.

We have lately heard of a remarkable instance of length of life in fish. Pausilypum[1] is the name of a villa in Campania, not far from Neapolis; here, as we learn from the works of M. Annsaus Seneca, a fish is known to have died sixty years after it had been placed in the preserves of C¾sar[2] by Vedius Pollio; while others of the same kind, and its equals in age, were living at the time that he wrote. This mention of fish-preserves reminds me that I ought to mention a few more particulars connected with this subject, before we leave the aquatic animals.

1. From the Greek tausi)lupon, "grief-assuaging." This was the name of a splendid villa belonging to Vedius Pollio, and which he bequeathed to Augustus. It was famous for its fish preserves; and it was here probably that Pollio kept his muren¾, previously mentioned by Pliny as being fed on human flesh. The vicinity is still called Monte Posilipo.

2. "C¾saris piscinis. "This may either mean, preserves which had their name from C¾sar, or preserves which afterwards belonged to C¾sar. The work of Seneca, in which this circumstance was mentioned, is no longer in existence.
